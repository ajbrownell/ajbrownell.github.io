Product Operating System
The system where what you learn shapes what you build — and what you build improves what you learn.
Poor product organizations have zero structure for consistently acting on insights — they depend on PM heroics.
Good product organizations run a pipeline — signals flow in, decisions and features flow out. This may appear to be effective initially, but the intelligence generated never compounds.
A great product organization closes the loop. Outcomes from decisions and new features create new signals and feedback. These signals form new insights, informing subsequent iterations. Every PM's discovery compounds and cross-pollinates. The full breadth of intelligence becomes available to every PM — across the full product suite.
An AI-native product organization goes further — every workflow feeds back intelligence, every AI agent has a defined job and a feedback path, and the operating system compounds faster than the market changes.
In a multi-product org the stakes are higher — complexity doesn't add linearly, it multiplies. Especially when AI capabilities are being embedded across every product layer simultaneously, often while still being treated as a separate product. Siloed assumptions don't just slow you down. They compound against you.
The architecture below is designed to close that gap.

Case Study 01
The Alignment Problem
System layer · Product Strategy · Makes the connection between feature requests and strategic outcomes mandatory, not assumed
Problem stated plainly
Teams work hard on things that don't advance the strategy. Not because they lack effort or intelligence, but because no mechanism exists that requires them to trace a feature request to a strategic outcome before work begins.
Why it matters
"When the link between new features and strategic outcomes is implicit, prioritization becomes political. The loudest voice wins. The operating model is just a dressed up org chart."
What made this non-obvious
The problem presents as a communication failure. Every diagnosis points to "we need better all-hands" or "strategy needs to be clearer." Both are wrong.

The real structure: communication fails because the artifact that initiates work contains no traceability requirement. There is no moment in the workflow where a PM must connect proposed work to a strategic outcome before the work begins. The connection is assumed, but almost never verified.

Traceability has to be structural, not cultural. Culture doesn't survive a leadership transition. Utility does.
Inspired — Cagan The JTBD Playbook — Kalbach Three Horizons — McKinsey Impact Mapping — Adzic
The design decision
The Capabilities to Outcomes Mapping Template. Two distinct flow types — causal design flow and outcome validation feedback — make the loop explicit and self-correcting. You cannot complete it without making every connection explicit — both the causal design and the feedback loop that validates it.

Outcome evidence

  • The system has outlasted the people who mandated it. That's the test.
  • $5M+ of proposed investment dollars repurposed to drive outcomes that mattered.
What I'd do differently
Piloted with willing partners — but not necessarily the most influential ones. Early adopters were enthusiastic but didn't carry the organizational weight to accelerate broader adoption. The result was a longer, slower scaling period than necessary. I'd now identify true champions from the start — people whose adoption others watch and follow, not just people who are open to trying something new.
"Willingness to adopt and ability to scale adoption are not the same thing."
Case Study 02
The Signal Problem
System layer · Product Insight · Each PM runs discovery in isolation — a shared commons turns isolated intelligence into org-wide shared capital
Problem stated plainly
Each PM runs their own discovery in isolation. Research cycles start from scratch every planning period — customer interviews, usage analysis, competitive signals — all custom-built per team, rarely shared, and typically discarded when the cycle ends. Across a multi-product org, five teams cover the same ground and reach different conclusions. The investment is duplicated. The intelligence is non-cumulative. The next PM starts from zero.
Why it matters
"Every PM doing their own discovery is a sign the org hasn't prioritized intelligence as a shared asset. The cost isn't just duplication — it's the compounding decisions being made from five different interpretations of the same market."
What made this non-obvious
The reflex is tooling: better dashboards, richer data, more reports. None of it addresses the structural problem — each PM's discovery is a private asset, running in parallel with every other team's identical effort and disappearing at the end of every cycle.

The real structure: the operating model was never designed to make intelligence cumulative. There is no mechanism that captures what one PM learns and makes it available to the next. Every cycle starts from zero — not because PMs are siloed by choice, but because the system has no memory.

Intelligence has to be shared by architecture, not by culture. Culture doesn't survive a planning cycle. A commons does.
Building Analytics Teams — Thompson Munger — signal vs noise Balfour — retention loops Continuous Discovery — Torres
The design decision
The Mission Intelligence Engine. The integrated knowledge-to-action platform of the operating model. Turns diverse, raw data into curated, continuously learning insight objects that feed action and measurable impact. Three orbiting capabilities around a central knowledge and orchestration core.
Three-capability architecture
D
Dovetail — Knowledge & Insight Curation
All qualitative and quantitative signals land in Dovetail first. AI-assisted tagging and human-in-the-loop review refine raw data into structured insight objects: tagged, themed, deduplicated, and linked to evidence.
G
Glean — Enterprise Search & Retrieval
Indexes Dovetail's curated insight objects continuously, making every validated finding discoverable via natural-language search at the point of decision, regardless of function.
P
ProductBoard — Insight-to-Roadmap Orchestration
Receives curated insight objects from Dovetail — never raw signals — and links them directly to features, themes, and product priorities. Syncs prioritized features to Jira for execution.
Foundation: Data Integration, Storage & Processing · Knowledge Graph · AI/ML · Orchestration
What was built
  • 1
    Mission Intelligence Engine
    Three-capability intelligence platform: Curation (Dovetail), Retrieval (Glean), Orchestration (ProductBoard → Jira). Each tool selected against a specific job-to-be-done. Sits above individual teams.
    ≈ Feedback Rivers — Rekhi
  • 2
    Insights to Outcomes Operating Model
    Signal coverage across five dimensions: Customer, Performance, Competitor, Market, and Future. Named owners per dimension.
    ≈ OODA Loop
  • 3
    Insights Hub + Persona Portal
    Shared intelligence commons with Discovery, Activation, and Foresight functions. 75+ continuously updated personas, journeys, blueprints, and competitor profiles — segmented by market, accessible across functions.
    ≈ Jobs Atlas — Kalbach
Outcome evidence
  • Planning moved from periodic to continuous. PMs shifted from periodic research sprints to working from a live intelligence layer — quarterly reviews now validate direction rather than set it from scratch.
  • The Insights Hub is the shared intelligence commons made real — with cross-functional access and zero parallel research cycles. Each PM's discovery compounds the intelligence available to every other PM, strategist, and marketer working from the same foundation.
  • The intelligence engine identified the Contact Center Consolidation market 12 months before opportunity volume materialized. The CCC Playbook was the GTM response. GSA GXCC was the first capture: a $100M floor Blanket Purchase Agreement with no ceiling. The market read was right. The system found it first.
What I'd do differently
I underestimated the willingness to share data and insights. The existing culture incentivized individual success, not shared accomplishments. If I were to do it again, I would first focus on the value obtained through consolidated, centralized data — before blurring that value with the value derived from sharing personal insights. By rolling it as a package deal, I created unnecessary friction that, in hindsight, could have been avoided.
"Understand current incentives before attempting change, then be sure to account for them."
Case Study 03
The Reinvention Problem
System layer · Product Execution · Turns knowledge into composable components — teams assemble, not reinvent
Problem stated plainly
Every product team solves the same problems from scratch. Not because they don't learn from each other — but because there's no architecture for reusing the solutions themselves. Onboarding flows, pricing models, integration patterns, instrumentation decisions — rebuilt by every team, against every new initiative, from first principles. The cost isn't just duplicated effort. It's inconsistent quality and solutions that reflect the team's current understanding, not the organization's accumulated one.
Why it matters
"Reinvention isn't just inefficient. It's a signal that the organization doesn't trust its own knowledge. Every from-scratch solution is a vote of no confidence in everything that came before it."
What made this non-obvious
The reinvention problem looks like a knowledge management problem. The reflex is documentation: capture what teams built, write up the decisions, store them somewhere central. None of it works. Documentation gets written and never retrieved. Wikis go stale.

The real structure: the problem isn't that knowledge isn't shared — it's that solutions aren't designed to be composable. A document describing how something was built is not a component that can be used again. You need an architecture for reuse, not a filing system for reference. The organization's accumulated work should be a building material, not a historical record.
Platform Revolution — Parker, Van Alstyne Atomic Design — Frost Component architecture — software engineering Inspired — Cagan
The design decision
Modular product architecture. Four layers, each with its own design standard, quality gate, and version history.
Layer 1ComponentsAtomic building blocks — one capability, one need
Layer 2FrameworksAssembly patterns for combining components
Layer 3PackagesAssembled from frameworks for specific contexts
Layer 4Shipped CapabilitiesFully deployed — assembled, not invented
The architecture doesn't prevent teams from building — it changes what building means. Teams stop designing from first principles and start composing from a shared foundation.
What was built
  • 1
    Component Repository
    Centralised, tagged, searchable library of reusable product components. Designed for composition and retrieval — not storage and reference.
    ≈ Design System
  • 2
    Contact Center Consolidation Playbook
    GTM response to the CCC market opportunity. Role-based navigation across eight decision domains, with pricing, risk, and phase roadmap built in — a product that closes deals, not a document that describes a solution.
    ≈ PRD
  • 3
    Use Case Evaluation Criteria & Library
    Emerging use cases by market and differentiation. Seven criteria connecting individual capability decisions to strategic market positioning — applied continuously.
    ≈ Opportunity Scoring
Outcome evidence
  • Visibility. For the first time, teams could see what was actively being built across the org. Features that would have been built in parallel — without either team knowing — were caught before duplication occurred. Awareness turned out to be more valuable than reuse.
  • Prevention. Fragmentation was stopped before it could form. The CCC Playbook established the pattern early enough that subsequent teams composed from a shared foundation rather than building their own version alongside it.
  • Discipline. Investment decisions improved measurably. The evaluation criteria replaced instinct-driven feature requests with an evidence standard — single-source justification no longer cleared the bar.
What I'd do differently
The Component Repository became a maintenance problem faster than expected — because we never defined ownership at the component level. Without clear owners, the volume quickly exceeded what a central team could sustain. The lesson wasn't to build better tooling. It was to resolve ownership before scaling the library. Every component needs a named owner before it ships — not as governance overhead, but as the minimum viable condition for it to remain trustworthy.
"A component without an owner is a component on borrowed time."
Case Study 04
The Adoption Problem
System layer · Outcomes → Signals feedback loop · Closes the loop — practitioners return to the system only when it earns that return
Problem stated plainly
Mandated systems die within a quarter. The pattern is consistent: leadership announces a new process, practitioners comply during rollout, usage drops, the mandate is quietly abandoned. The system gets blamed. The real problem was the adoption model — specifically, that compliance was mistaken for adoption and borrowed trust for earned utility.
Why it matters
"Mandated adoption is borrowed trust. It runs out the moment the mandate author leaves the room. The only durable adoption is earned — where practitioners use the system because it makes their work better, not because someone is watching."
What made this non-obvious
Adoption looks like a change management problem. The reflex: run more training, communicate better, find more champions. None of it works if the system requires behavior change before delivering value. You're asking practitioners to invest before they've seen a return. That's not a change management failure — it's a sequencing failure.

The real structure: value has to come first. Trust follows utility. The system has to solve a problem the practitioner feels today — not a problem leadership wants solved eventually.

Compliance isn't adoption. They produce identical-looking metrics for about six weeks. Then they diverge completely.
Munger — incentive before instruction Kotter — change sequencing Fogg Behavior Model — Fogg Product-Led Growth — Bush
The design decision
Five-phase rollout model built around value sequencing, not process sequencing.
1
Diagnose
Find the felt pain, not the assumed pain. Interview before designing.
The system has to solve what practitioners actually experience, not what leadership thinks they experience.
2
Co-Create
Practitioners are architects of the system, not recipients of it.
PMs who build the standard own it. PMs who receive it comply with it.
3
Pilot
One clean before/after story that travels. Proof before scale.
4
Champion Network
PM-to-PM adoption is faster and more durable than top-down rollout.
Peer credibility outlasts executive mandate.
5
Institutionalize
Embed in onboarding. Make the right way the easy way.
What was built
  • 1
    Co-Creation Workshops
    Structured sessions that did four jobs simultaneously: diagnosed felt pain rather than assumed pain, surfaced the existing incentive structure, identified natural champions, and transferred ownership to practitioners before the system was designed. The workshop output wasn't a document — it was a coalition.
    ≈ Discovery Sprint
  • 2
    Champion-First Rollout Model
    Champions identified during co-creation were sequenced into the rollout before broader release. Not because they were enthusiastic — because their peers watched them. Influential practitioners adopting voluntarily carried more weight than any mandate could.
    ≈ Diffusion of Innovations — Rogers
  • 3
    Incentive Mapping
    Before designing what practitioners were being asked to do differently, the existing incentive structure was mapped. The system was then designed to deliver value before asking for behavior change — advocacy was the outcome of value delivery, not a precondition for it.
    ≈ Behavior Design — Fogg
Outcome evidence
  • 75+ practitioners adopted voluntarily across five portfolios. Zero mandates. The co-creation model meant practitioners weren't recipients of the system — they were its architects. Ownership preceded rollout.
  • Peer adoption proved faster and more durable than top-down rollout. Champions identified during workshops became the adoption engine. Credibility traveled horizontally, not vertically.
  • The system survived two executive leadership transitions without a reset. Not because it was mandated — because practitioners found it useful enough to protect. Nobody defended the system. They defended what it enabled them to do. That's the only version of a lasting mark that holds.
What I'd do differently
Scheduled retrospectives quarterly when early adoption needed monthly. By the time feedback arrived, patterns were entrenched. I'd now run a five-minute pulse check every two weeks in the first quarter — not to evaluate the system, but to catch drift before it compounds.
"Early adoption is when the system is most fragile and the feedback loop is most critical. Those two facts are in direct tension."
Case Study 05
The AI Augmentation Problem
System layer · Intelligence Compounding · The first four problems build the foundation. This is what becomes possible when that foundation is in place.
Problem stated plainly
Most organizations adopt AI generically — tools layered on unresolved process and disconnected data. Each team runs its own experiments. Each experiment optimizes for local efficiency. None of it compounds. The org gets faster at the wrong things, and the intelligence each AI workflow generates stays trapped inside the team that deployed it — never feeding the operating system, never informing the next decision, never becoming organizational advantage. Not generic AI adoption. A designed intelligence layer where every agent has a specific job, a defined data source, and a measurable output that feeds the next layer of the arc.
Why it matters
"AI doesn't replace the operating system — it accelerates it. Deploy AI on a broken operating model and you get faster fragmentation. Deploy it on a functioning one and you get a system that gets smarter every cycle. The difference isn't the model. It's the architecture underneath."
What made this non-obvious
The failure mode presents as a tooling problem. Teams try different models, swap vendors, add guardrails. None of it addresses the structural issue — AI is being adopted as a capability, not designed as an architecture.

The deeper problem is sequencing. Most organizations jump straight to building agents — skipping the readiness assessment that tells you if the data and process foundations exist, the workflow mapping that tells you where agents will actually reduce friction, and the operating model design that defines how humans and agents work together. An agent strategy without those foundations is just a faster way to automate the wrong things.

A running agent that is not governed degrades. Without review rhythms and a feedback architecture, the intelligence layer becomes stale and loses trust. Governance has to be designed before deployment — not retrofitted after drift.
Accelerate — Forsgren, Humble, Kim NIST AI Risk Management Framework IBM AI Value Creators Framework
The design decision
A sequenced arc, not a single deployment. Six problems in order — each one creates the conditions the next one requires. No agent is designed until the conditions are understood. No agent is scaled until it's governed.
AG-1AI Readiness AssessmentData maturity, workflow structure, decision architecture
AG-2Workflow & Decision MappingWhere agents reduce friction or surface insight
AG-3Agent Strategy & ArchitectureJob definitions, data sources, output specs, sequencing
AG-4AI Operating Model DesignRoles, governance structure, human-agent interaction
AG-5Agent Design & PilotOne running agent. Proof before scale.
AG-6Intelligence System Governance & ScaleFeedback architecture, iteration protocol, scale roadmap
Each module creates the conditions the next one requires. AG-6 is what makes the system get smarter every cycle.
What was built
  • 1
    AI Readiness Assessment & Workflow Map
    Honest audit across data maturity, workflow structure, team capability, and decision architecture — establishing what was ready, what was not, and what each subsequent deployment required before any agent was designed.
    ≈ AG-1 · AG-2
  • 2
    Agent Strategy & Architecture — Glean + CoPilot
    Full agent stack with job definitions, data source mapping, output specifications, and priority sequencing. Glean deployed as the enterprise retrieval layer — making the full intelligence commons searchable at the point of decision. CoPilot agents embedded into delivery rhythms with defined jobs, data sources, and measurable outputs.
    ≈ AG-3
  • 3
    AI Operating Model & Governance Framework
    Built in a FedRAMP-authorized environment — roles, governance structure, human-agent interaction design, escalation paths, evaluation criteria, and confidence thresholds defined before deployment. Not a compliance layer — a design requirement. Every agent has a named owner, a review cadence, and a feedback path back into the operating system.
    ≈ AG-4 · AG-6
  • 4
    Intelligence System Governance & Scale
    Feedback architecture ensuring every agent output becomes a signal for the next design cycle. Iteration protocol for measuring, reviewing, and improving agent performance. Scale roadmap for sequencing remaining agents against the priority stack — so the system compounds rather than sprawls.
    ≈ AG-6
Outcome evidence
  • AI workflows compound rather than drift. Governance designed before deployment means every output is validated against defined criteria — exceptions feed back as signal rather than disappearing as noise.
  • Intelligence became retrievable at the point of decision. Glean indexed the full intelligence commons — eliminating the gap between what the organization had learned and what practitioners could access when it mattered.
  • Human judgment stayed in the loop without slowing the system. Defining override protocols and confidence thresholds before deployment meant practitioners trusted the outputs — because they understood exactly where the system acted autonomously and where it deferred to them.
  • Deployed in a FedRAMP-authorized environment — proving the architecture holds under compliance, security, and audit requirements that commercial deployments rarely face. The governance model is more rigorous, not less.
What I'd do differently
Underestimated how much the sequencing lesson needed to be taught, not just applied. The arc from readiness through governance is obvious in hindsight — but most stakeholders arrive wanting to start at AG-5. The readiness assessment felt like delay to people who wanted to see a running agent. I'd now make the cost of skipping steps visible earlier — not as a gate, but as a shared understanding of what gets compounded and what gets lost depending on where you start.
"The organizations that get this right don't just use AI. They build systems that get smarter every cycle."
Operating Principles
Three principles. Every context. Same results.
Shaped through years of iteration and tested under pressure.
Products reflect systems.
The product you ship is a direct expression of the organizational system that produced it. Misaligned teams build misaligned products. Siloed intelligence produces products that don't learn. Reactive operating models produce features that don't compound. You cannot build a better product without first building a better system. Trying to fix the product without fixing the operating system is the most common and most expensive mistake in product operations.
Outcomes before capabilities.
A capability without a traced outcome is a guess wearing a roadmap. Before any capability is designed, the outcome it enables must be named, the pain point it addresses must be verified, and the metric that will confirm it worked must be agreed upon. This is not a planning ritual — it's the forcing function that separates strategy from activity. Organizations that skip this step don't lack execution. They lack direction.
Ownership determines durability.
Mandated systems survive until the mandate changes. Systems practitioners own because they're useful survive leadership transitions, reorgs, and budget cycles. The only operating model worth building is one practitioners would protect voluntarily — not because they're required to, but because losing it would make them worse at their jobs. If that condition isn't met, the system isn't done yet.
